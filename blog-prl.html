Positive Reinforcement Learning

- Improving the traditional classroom learning

  • June 30, 2021

Not long back, our schools were full of stern words, discipline and punishments. It was commonplace for teachers to favour harsh punishment over positive punishment. Now, however, it is recognized that there are more effective ways to teach and to manage classrooms, such as positive reinforcement.

What is Positive Reinforcement Learning ?

Positive reinforcement was introduced by B.F. Skinner. It is a form of learning whereby the when a specific behaviour is performed and when a desirable consequence occurs, it helps increase the likelihood of that behaviour recurring.

When thinking about positive reinforcement in teaching and education, the purpose is to provide an incentive for students to repeat desired behaviours. In other words, by providing students with a positive outcome when they accomplish achievements or display certain behaviours, students are encouraged to do so again.

5 types of Positive Reinforcement in the Classroom

1. Direct reinforcement

This refers to a type of reinforcement that, as the name suggests, directly results from the appropriate behaviour. Direct reinforcement is one way to make changes to the environment that influences behaviour.

Example :
• If a child interacts appropriately with their peers in a group activity, this will most likely lead to further invitations to join in on such activities in future.
• If a child studies and prepares for a test and gets the highest marks, it would lead to studying the same for future tests.

2. Social reinforcement

Social reinforcement refers to reinforcers such as smiles, acceptance, praise, acclaim, and attention from other people. These are mediated by others such as; teachers, parents, other adults, peers. They involve an expression of approval and praise for appropriate behaviour.

Example :
• Positive and appreciative comments.
• Written approval (e.g. writing ‘super’ on a completed worksheet).
• Other expressions of approval (such as smiling, nodding your head, clapping, a pat on the back).

3. Activity reinforcement

Involves allowing students to take part in their preferred activities if they behave appropriately. This is especially effective if they are allowed to choose a classmate with whom they can, for example, play a game or spend time on the computer with. This provides social reinforcement from their partner, too.

4. Tangible reinforcement

For example; toys, balloons, badges, stickers, and awards. Awards such as certificates, displaying work in the classroom, or a letter sent home to parents praising students’ progress can be used as reinforcement. By giving a physical reward, it makes other students envious and want to earn it as well by repeating the same behaviour.

5. Token reinforcement

Occurs when points or tokens are awarded for appropriate behaviour. The rewards themselves have little value but they can be collected, then exchanged for something valuable to the student. For example, every time a student shows a certain behaviour, the teacher could give them a ticket. At the end of the week, tickets can be exchanged for a prize.

Benefits of using Positive Reinforcement Learning

• Students can learn through the social cues of their teachers as to what constitutes acceptable behaviour. For example, if one student is praised for a desired behaviour, other students also learn that this behaviour is acceptable and that it will be praised.

• One important advantage of using positive reinforcement is that students actively enjoy being present and learning in the classroom.

• It allows accomplishment to be celebrated as a class.

• Positive reinforcement leads to a greater sense of community in the class.

• Use of positive reinforcement is related to increased student attendance.

• When positive reinforcement is used, students are more motivated and proactive.

• Use of positive reinforcement leads to heightened enthusiasm in students – and even the teacher!
